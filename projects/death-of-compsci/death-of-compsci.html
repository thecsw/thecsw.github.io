February 25th, 2026

When it comes to AI, I quite dislike it. You could even say hate it. On every possible level. I find it vile to what it does to our minds, bodies, and how we see and treat each other. It’s fundamentally the single most influential across-the-board technology we have ever discovered and decided to pursue blindly.

Financially, it’s prohibitively expensive for the regular person to use whether through paying for at the moment VC-subsidized subscriptions or acquiring the hardware needed to run the LLMs locally at a fast enough inference speed that it makes sense.

Environmentally, it’s killing our forests, heating up the planet, the data centers burn the land, violate the nature, poison our water, and in turn, also have severely negative effects on our bodies for anyone nearby one of them.

Societally, it’s a grifter’s economy, where anybody with the smallest amount of skillset and ample of free time is happy to abuse the technology to take advantage of their fellow man. Number 1 usage of AI is for mental health, like that is not already in crisis of in itself, and AI sycophantically supporting everything you say is not good.

Ethically, it blurs the line for people between human-made and machine-made, resulting in a never-ending slopification of all things we hold dear and that had for tens of thousands of years made us human—art, music, painting, cartoon, filmmaking, gaming. Verbatim regurgitation of copyrighted works. Displacement of creative endeavors.

Professionally, it’s impoverishing the talent and robbing us of our skills that we had spent years, decades honing, training, and taking pride in. We are getting stuck in an ouroboros setup, where with junior talent getting squeezed, all the people who “were in” will die or retire, if they ever get a chance to get to the latter.

In spite of it all, when it comes down to it, to my day to day life, I quite love AI. I use it daily, we all use it daily. If you were to ask me if I’d give it up to go back to my daily life as a software engineer, my answer would be an astounding no. We use it to such an extent that we even joke—but actually just saying the truth, so we don’t out ourselves too much—without AI, if work doesn’t come to a complete halt, it will be a fraction of what we are doing now, unless we go and kill ourselves by getting that LED monitor tan 24/7.

Why is that? Why is it that I find it so vile yet can’t go back. It’s not like it’s illegal, like some hard drug you got from John over the street. It’s not a forbidden fruit you’ve been told not to touch, otherwise you’ll get banished to Hell, on the contrary, your performance and bonuses rely on you using it. And when it comes to using it, it makes life, when used properly, so much easier.

In my tenure as a software engineer, I have always—without ego nor some cursed form of self-congratulating—saw myself as a high-performing engineer. Not because I worked excessively—though I did work pretty standard hours—but because I focused and would lock in, which helped me to enjoy a good share of visibility, promotions, and awards.

Yet now, I’m delivering even more, more than ever, that comparing me doing something now to then, and me then now, I would get a sit-down with my manager over my performance and if I have had something happen in my life that is affecting the throughput of my work.

If I ever bring these concerns with my friends or other people in the industry, they’d immediately retort, “Yeah Sandy, do you also hate cars and modern medicine?”

In the process of thinking and living through it, I’ve come to a harsh truth realization of it, specifically in the context of software engineering and the industry that we decided to devote ourselves to—at least right now, here, today—not so sure about tomorrow.

As a little background about me, I sat exactly where you are 4 years ago. Graduated in 2022, did three majors, decided to go work for Microsoft. At 3.5 years, reached the coveted Senior title and left a month after that to work for a stealth AI company. Stealth means that we are working away from the public’s eye, pre-GA product, GA stands for General Availability, meaning whatever we sell is for you to buy.

Working at an AI-first company, even from Microsoft, who had reach $4T market valuation, in only thanks to the massive AI push and OpenAI partnership, essentially, selling shovels in this gold rush, what we do know is moving so fast that in a week we collectively do more than we could at Microsoft in half a year, and the people I work with, we are all ex-Microsoft. Same people.

We get to enjoy to be frightened of what is to come, since we witness things a few months, maybe years in advance, before they reach all corners of our profession. In all the flame wars, on hackernews, in the streets, across the halls of LEEP2 and Eaton—is AI truly replacing us and coming after our jobs? The answer is yes. Is there no art to what we do anymore, no place for a human? There is none.

It comes to the crucial distinction that we must make between Programming and Software Engineering.

Programming of in itself, is us learning how to code, obsessing over the smallest pieces of code, finding beauty in the structures we build, order in the algorithms we employ, poetry in how every single piece falls into its place, and that we did, what we did, is so uniquely ours, that we marvel at it. We did it. We solved it, and reached the promised land with the power of code.

In the end, it is quite useless. That is the beauty of it. We may not make any money out of it, we might leave it unchanged for years and decades. But we will obsess over it. One of my proudest projects is building a website builder. It’s been around for 6-7 years now, continuously maintained, some odd 10k LOC in go, and I love it. I use it daily, write my website in it, but what matters to me is not that it works, but how much I’ve learned throughout it.

Or when I learned how to use Donald Knuth’s CWEB and got infected with the idea of Literate Programming, which once and forever changed how I see programming completely, not as a means to an end of explaining code to a machine and make it go brrr, but writing a work of literature for my fellow brother, for someone else to read and marvel at it as I do.

In Picture of Dorian Gray, Oscar Wilde says in the Preface,

We can forgive a man for making a useful thing as long as he does not admire it. The only excuse for making a useless thing is that one admires it intensely.

All art is quite useless.

That is art. We give it meaning, through the process of doing it, through learning, failing, and loving it. Through the attention to detail and experiencing all sorts of emotions throughout. Yet, we live in a capitalistic society that has no care for that and the brutal reality, to the dismay and disappointment of Ancient Romans, who would find it vulgar to how we have to work to survive—we have to work to afford life and to exist, and software engineering is what gets us there.

In contrast to programming, where we talk of the journey and the in-betweens, software engineering primarily and solely devotes its attention to the final product. It doesn’t have to be beautiful or elegant or perfect, but it does have to be good enough, which AI today, even if it all goes up in flames and the bubble pops, deafening everyone in vicinity, as it is right now, even the open source models, the AI does it very very well.

The boss, the business, doesn’t care whether this new Java service that handles the checkout flow for the customers is written with AI and runs the same as what the human would, but the former taking a few minutes vs the human taking weeks to do so—the cost benefit analysis an executive would do in their heads puts the human in the loop in a disadvantageous position.

The models that exist today and the skillsets that senior-plus engineers had already developed in working with them, the produced code would be of the similar if not the same quality as the person would write, but created in a fraction of a time and even smaller fraction of pain and trial and error that it takes to get there. It is true, because we see it, I see it daily.

In programming, we would work on all these algorithms of traversing graphs as quickly as possible and all the math behind it, in software engineering, even the most naive approach would do just fine, when the scale and scenario are taken into account, and again, AI is great at that, since it devoured all of that information, and it knows what libraries to use, even if it doesn’t, it can autonomously experiment, just as a human would, but with the human now out of the picture completely.

Even if we stick to our ideals and say that we will do it ourselves—we cannot forget how competitive our profession is. In college, we all go towards the same goal, and the University is incentivized to graduate as many students as possible, which works with our goal of doing as little amount of work as possible and to still graduate.

In the “real world” of working for a company, the reality is more cut-throat. You will be surrounded by career people, psychopaths, 10x-100x engineers, bureaucrats, back-stabbers, such that even if the company says there are no heroes and we all work together to reach the company’s goal—as it should be—the company will always reward people for the extra effort.

The ones with maximum impact and throughput, will get the bonuses, and promotions, and some-sort of job stability (never a guarantee), and it is easier to get there and be that when you have such powerful tooling like AI, which can save you hours per day when doing real corporate work, like writing another Helm Chart, writing another Github/ADO workflow, addressing another product manager’s JIRA ticket in the codebase, implementing another unit or integration test.

If you don’t do it, then someone else around you, in your team, the pragmatic engineer with no qualms of rushing forward will do it. In this way, triggering and fueling the AI arms race amongst the software engineers. Is it a rat race? Yes. Is it a race t the bottom, de-skilling ourselves and producing possibly hollow work? Yes. Is there a way out of it? I don’t see it, at the moment.

Unless you get rich, never have to work a day because your dividends from stocks are doing all the hard work, and you can finally enjoy an Ancient Roman’s “otium”—spending time on things that life is meant for: hanging out with friends, reading fulfilling literature, listening to good music, bettering yourself as a human, and most importantly, drinking great wine.
